The Handshaking Lemma and Graph Traversal

April 5, 2026 · 8 min read

Graph theory is one of those mathematical disciplines where deceptively simple statements reveal deep structural truths. The Handshaking Lemma is a perfect example: a result you can prove in two lines, yet one that sits at the heart of algorithms from breadth-first search to graph neural network message passing.

Basics: what is a graph?

Formally, a graph $G = (V, E)$ consists of a finite set of vertices $V$ and a set of edges $E \subseteq \binom{V}{2}$. Each edge connects two distinct vertices. For a vertex $v \in V$, its degree $\deg(v)$ counts how many edges are incident to it:

$$\deg(v) = |\{e \in E : v \in e\}|$$

A graph with $n$ vertices and $m$ edges will appear throughout this post as $G = (V, E)$ with $|V| = n$ and $|E| = m$.

The Handshaking Lemma

Theorem (Handshaking Lemma). For any undirected graph $G = (V, E)$,

$$\sum_{v \in V} \deg(v) = 2|E|$$

Proof. Each edge $\{u, v\} \in E$ contributes exactly $1$ to $\deg(u)$ and $1$ to $\deg(v)$, hence exactly $2$ to the total degree sum. Summing over all edges gives $2|E|$. $\square$

An immediate corollary is that in any graph, the number of vertices with odd degree must be even. This is not just a curiosity — it is the combinatorial backbone of Eulerian circuit existence.

Euler's criterion for Eulerian circuits

A connected graph $G$ admits an Eulerian circuit (a closed walk traversing every edge exactly once) if and only if every vertex has even degree. Using the Handshaking Lemma, this immediately implies that the total number of edges must satisfy:

$$m = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{v \in V} \deg(v), \quad \text{with all } \deg(v) \text{ even}$$

For a path (not circuit) version, exactly two vertices are allowed to have odd degree — these become the start and end of the Eulerian path.

Directed graphs and in/out-degrees

In a directed graph (digraph) $G = (V, A)$ with arc set $A$, each arc $a = (u, v)$ contributes $1$ to the out-degree of $u$ and $1$ to the in-degree of $v$. The analogous identity is:

$$\sum_{v \in V} \deg^+(v) = \sum_{v \in V} \deg^-(v) = |A|$$

where $\deg^+(v)$ is the out-degree and $\deg^-(v)$ the in-degree of $v$. This identity is essential when analysing flow networks and Markov chains.

Connection to graph neural networks

In a Graph Neural Network (GNN), each layer aggregates messages from a node's neighbourhood. For a node $v$, the standard message-passing update is:

$$\mathbf{h}_v^{(k)} = \phi\!\left(\mathbf{h}_v^{(k-1)},\; \bigoplus_{u \in \mathcal{N}(v)} \psi\!\left(\mathbf{h}_u^{(k-1)}\right)\right)$$

where $\mathcal{N}(v)$ is the neighbourhood of $v$, $\phi$ and $\psi$ are learnable functions, and $\bigoplus$ is a permutation-invariant aggregator (sum, mean, or max). The total number of messages exchanged per layer across the entire graph is exactly $\sum_{v} \deg(v) = 2m$ — a direct consequence of the Handshaking Lemma. This is why GNN complexity is typically stated as $O(m)$ per layer.

Breadth-first search complexity

BFS explores a graph layer by layer. In each step, it processes a vertex $v$ and enqueues all unvisited neighbours. The total work is proportional to:

$$\sum_{v \in V} \deg(v) = 2m = O(m)$$

Combined with the $O(n)$ work for initialising the visited array, BFS runs in $O(n + m)$ — optimal for sparse graphs where $m \ll n^2$.

A Python example

The following snippet verifies the Handshaking Lemma empirically using networkx: 

import networkx as nx

G = nx.erdos_renyi_graph(n=100, p=0.05, seed=42)

total_degree = sum(d for _, d in G.degree())
num_edges    = G.number_of_edges()

assert total_degree == 2 * num_edges, "Handshaking Lemma violated!"
print(f"Vertices : {G.number_of_nodes()}")
print(f"Edges    : {num_edges}")
print(f"Σ deg(v) : {total_degree}  (= 2 × {num_edges})")

Running this on a random Erdős–Rényi graph with $n = 100$ nodes and edge probability $p = 0.05$ will always satisfy the assertion, regardless of the random seed.

Summary

The Handshaking Lemma is one of graph theory's foundational identities. Its proof is trivial, but its consequences cascade through combinatorics, algorithm analysis, and modern machine learning on graphs. Understanding it deeply helps build intuition for why graph algorithms scale the way they do — and why GNN complexity is measured in edge counts rather than vertex counts.

The best results in mathematics are those where the proof is shorter than the applications.
