The Beauty and Power of Mathematics

April 11, 2026 · 12 min read

When I was at high school, I was somewhat uninterested in mathematics. Sure, I was good at it, but I did not find it particularly captivating. Back then I was more drawn to literature — writing short stories and reading books. Mathematics felt like a collection of rules to memorise for exams, nothing more.

That changed dramatically when I started my university journey and enrolled in computer science. Studying computer science is not just about programming cool user interfaces and websites, or learning a new tool every other week. It is also about studying the fundamental principles of computation and information processing, analysing the limitations of algorithms, working with efficient data structures, and understanding the mappings between human- and machine-readable representations of the world — and so much more. There is one thing that is, explicitly or implicitly, present everywhere in the computing world: mathematics.

Completing several courses in higher mathematics during my degree is what ignited my passion. I stopped thinking about mathematics as a set of equations to memorise in order to pass a test. I began to understand what mathematics is really about — and it is far more interesting than I had ever imagined. It is about structure and abstraction. Mathematics is not about solving formulas; it is about discovering universal truths that remain true regardless of the physical world. And the most fascinating part? Its unreasonable effectiveness at describing and modelling that very world.

Without mathematics, we as a species would probably never have accomplished what we have. We are a technological — and therefore, in a very literal sense, a mathematical — civilisation. Think about it: buildings, bridges, cars, aeroplanes, CT scanners, microscopes, computers, smartphones, software, the entire energy infrastructure, even the glasses on your face — none of it would exist without mathematics. It is the invisible scaffolding holding the modern world together.

I want to demonstrate the power of mathematics on a concrete and surprisingly elegant example: web search. Specifically, the algorithm that made Google, Google — PageRank.

1. The Problem: Finding a Needle in a Billion Haystacks

It is 1998. The web has roughly 2.4 million pages and is doubling every few months. How do you find anything? Yahoo had human editors manually categorising links — and the approach was already collapsing under its own weight.

The question Larry Page and Sergey Brin asked was deceptively simple:

Which pages are important?

The naive answer — count visits — requires data you do not have. Their insight was more elegant: the web itself already contains the answer, encoded in its link structure. A link from page $A$ to page $B$ is a vote of confidence. But not all votes are equal — a vote from an important page is worth more than a vote from an obscure one.

This seems circular at first glance. Importance depends on who links to you, which depends on their importance, which depends on who links to them... The problem looks like it leads to infinite regress. And yet, mathematics dissolves the circularity completely.

2. Why It Mattered

This was not an academic exercise. Search was the chokepoint of the entire internet economy. Whoever solved relevance would effectively control how humanity navigates information. The challenge had three distinct layers:

  • Scale — billions of pages, no central authority.
  • Manipulation — any naive metric gets gamed immediately by spammers.
  • Circularity — importance is self-referential by nature.

What Page and Brin needed was not just a ranking formula. They needed a model of human behaviour that would be hard to fake, globally consistent, and computationally tractable. Link-counting collapses under spam. Click-tracking requires surveillance infrastructure that did not exist. But structure — the graph of who points to whom — is objective, observable, and deeply meaningful. And structure is precisely what mathematics is built to handle.

3. Mathematisation: From Chaos to Structure

3.1 The Web as a Graph

The first step is radical abstraction. Forget content, language, images, layout — everything. What remains? Only relationships. We model the web as a directed graph:

$$G = (V, E)$$

where $V$ is the set of pages (nodes) and $E$ is the set of hyperlinks (directed edges). A link from page $A$ to page $B$ corresponds to a directed edge $A \to B$. The direction reflects the one-way nature of hyperlinks. We also care about the so called out-degree $d_i = \deg^+(v_i)$, the number of outgoing links from page $i$.

This abstraction discards an enormous amount of information. And yet, as we are about to see, it discards nothing that matters for the question of importance.

3.2 The Stochastic Transition Matrix

Define the adjacency matrix $A \in \{0,1\}^{n \times n}$ where:

$$A_{ij} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if page } i \text{ links to page } j \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Now normalise each row by the out-degree $d_i = \sum_j A_{ij}$:

$$M_{ij} = \frac{A_{ij}}{d_i}$$

This produces the row-stochastic transition matrix $M$. Each entry $M_{ij}$ is the probability of jumping from page $i$ to page $j$. Each row sums to 1. The web has been transformed into a probability machine.

3.3 The Random Walk

Here the model earns its elegance. Forget "importance" entirely — think instead of a random surfer: a person who starts on a random page, reads it, then clicks a uniformly random outgoing link, forever. Where does this person spend most of their time?

Let $\mathbf{r}^{(t)} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ be a probability vector where $r^{(t)}_i$ is the probability of the surfer being on page $i$ at time $t$. The surfer's position evolves as:

$$\mathbf{r}^{(t+1)} = M^T \mathbf{r}^{(t)}$$

Each step redistributes probability mass across the graph according to the link structure. Think of it as a fluid flowing through pipes: the pipes are the hyperlinks, and the fluid is probability.

3.4 The Damping Factor — Making the Model Realistic

Two technical problems arise. Dangling nodes (pages with no outgoing links) trap the surfer, and spider traps (closed cycles) absorb all probability mass and never release it. The fix is both practical and elegant: at each step, with probability $\alpha$ the surfer follows a random outgoing link, and with probability $(1 - \alpha)$ they teleport to a completely random page on the web:

$$G = \alpha M^T + (1-\alpha)\, \frac{\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^T}{n}$$

This is the Google Matrix. Page and Brin used $\alpha \approx 0.85$. The teleportation term $\frac{\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^T}{n}$ ensures every page is reachable from every other — the graph becomes strongly connected and aperiodic, which is exactly what we need for the next step.

4. The Solution: A Stationary Distribution

4.1 The Fixed-Point Equation

We want a ranking vector $\mathbf{r}^*$ that does not change when we apply another step of the random walk — a distribution the surfer settles into permanently. That condition is:

$$\mathbf{r}^* = G\,\mathbf{r}^*$$

This is an eigenvector equation. We are looking for the eigenvector of $G$ corresponding to eigenvalue $\lambda = 1$. The circular definition of importance has been rewritten as a fixed point of a linear map. The infinite regress is gone.

4.2 Existence and Uniqueness — Perron–Frobenius

Does such a vector exist? Is it unique? The Perron–Frobenius theorem answers both questions. For any matrix with strictly positive entries — which $G$ is, thanks to the teleportation term — there exists a unique dominant real eigenvalue $\lambda_1 = 1$, and its corresponding eigenvector has all strictly positive components:

$$G\mathbf{r}^* = 1 \cdot \mathbf{r}^*, \quad r^*_i > 0 \quad \forall\, i$$

The mathematics guarantees precisely what the application demands: a unique, well-defined, strictly positive ranking of every page on the web. No ambiguity, no degenerate cases.

4.3 Computing It — Power Iteration

Computing eigenvectors of a billion-node matrix analytically is, of course, out of the question. But it turns out you do not need to. Start from any probability distribution $\mathbf{r}^{(0)}$ — say, uniform — and repeatedly apply:

$$\mathbf{r}^{(t+1)} = G\,\mathbf{r}^{(t)}$$

By Perron–Frobenius, this sequence always converges to $\mathbf{r}^*$, regardless of the starting point. The convergence rate is governed by the ratio $\frac{\lambda_2}{\lambda_1}$ of the two largest eigenvalues. In practice, Google's original implementation converged in roughly 50–100 iterations over the entire web.

4.4 What the Ranking Means

The stationary distribution carries a beautifully concrete interpretation: $r^*_i$ is the fraction of time the random surfer spends on page $i$ in the long run. No circular definition. No chicken-and-egg problem. Just a river of probability finding its natural level across the topology of human knowledge.

5. The Beauty of It

Step back and look at the full arc of what happened:

$$\text{human curiosity} \;\to\; \text{hyperlinks} \;\to\; \text{directed graph} \;\to\; \text{stochastic matrix} \;\to\; \text{eigenvector}$$

A messy, billion-node, chaotic human artefact was abstracted into a matrix, and the answer emerged as its dominant eigenvector. A few things are worth pausing on.

The abstraction discards everything and loses nothing. Page content, language, visual design, topic — all stripped away. What remains is pure relational structure, and that structure alone is sufficient to rank the web better than any team of human editors could.

The solution was already inside the problem. Nobody imposed a ranking from the outside. The PageRank vector is intrinsic to the graph — it emerges from the link structure itself, the way a river's course emerges from the landscape's topology. The random surfer does not create importance; they reveal it.

It connects ideas from completely different eras. Markov chains (19th-century probability theory), the Perron–Frobenius theorem (early 20th-century linear algebra), and graph theory all crystallise together to answer a question that did not exist until 1995. Mathematics does not care about chronology. Tools built centuries apart slot together perfectly when the right problem arrives.

The eternal truth angle. The dominant eigenvector of a stochastic matrix was a mathematical fact long before hyperlinks existed — long before computers, long before the internet. PageRank did not invent a new truth. It recognised that an ancient truth was the right language for a new problem.

That is what mathematical beauty usually looks like. Not a magic trick. A homecoming.
